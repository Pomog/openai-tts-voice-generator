Now we use Newton’s second law.

For an object with constant mass, force equals mass times acceleration.

Work becomes the integral of mass times acceleration dotted with velocity over time.

Acceleration is the change of velocity with respect to time.

So the expression becomes mass times the integral of the velocity derivative dotted with velocity over time.

Now comes the key step.

During a tiny time step, acceleration creates a tiny change in velocity.

So acceleration multiplied by a tiny time step gives a tiny change in velocity.

This lets us change the integral from time to velocity.

For straight-line motion, where acceleration and velocity point along the same line, the vector expression becomes simpler.

Acceleration dotted with velocity becomes speed multiplied by the rate of change of speed.

So the work becomes mass times the integral of v d v.

Now this integral is simple.

The integral of v d v is one half v squared.

So the work becomes one half mass times final velocity squared, minus one half mass times starting velocity squared.

This means work is the change in kinetic energy.

So calculus gives us the formula for kinetic energy.

Work transferred by force becomes motion stored as kinetic energy.
