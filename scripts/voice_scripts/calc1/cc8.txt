Now we simplify the integral.

G is constant.

The masses are constant.

Only r changes.

So we can move G, M, and m outside the integral.

Then we get.

W g equals minus G M m multiplied by the integral from r zero to r f of one divided by r squared, d r.

Now comes the calculus step.

One divided by r squared is the same as r to the power minus two.

Using the power rule, the integral of r to the power minus two is minus one divided by r.

So the integral gives us minus one over r.

Now we apply the limits.

First, we use the final distance r f.

Then we subtract the value at the starting distance r zero.

After applying the limits, the result becomes.

W g equals G M m multiplied by one over r f minus one over r zero.

So the work done by gravity is.

W g equals G M m, open bracket, one over final distance minus one over starting distance, close bracket.

Now suppose the asteroid starts very far away.

Mathematically, we say that the starting distance r zero approaches infinity.

And suppose the asteroid falls down to the planet’s surface.

Then the final center-to-center distance is approximately the planet radius, capital R.

So the formula becomes.

W g equals G M m multiplied by one over R minus one over r zero.

As r zero approaches infinity, one over r zero approaches zero.

So the work done by gravity becomes.

W g equals G M m divided by R.

This work is positive.

That makes sense.

When the asteroid falls inward, gravity gives energy to the asteroid.

The asteroid speeds up.

This formula is the bridge between gravitational interaction and motion.

To get the formula for speed in a gravitational field, we now connect this gravitational work to kinetic energy.
