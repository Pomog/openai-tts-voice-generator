Now let us compare this speed with something familiar.

A passenger airplane usually flies at about nine hundred kilometers per hour.

That is about zero point two five kilometers per second.

But our calculated speed is about eleven point one eight kilometers per second.

So the falling asteroid would be more than forty times faster than a passenger airplane.

This is far above Mach five, the usual boundary for hypersonic speed.

Mach five is roughly one point seven kilometers per second, depending on altitude and air temperature.

At hypersonic speed, the air in front of the object cannot move away fast enough.

The gas is compressed extremely quickly.

This rapid compression is close to adiabatic compression, meaning the gas heats up before it can transfer heat away.

At eleven kilometers per second, this heating becomes extreme.

A strong shock wave forms in front of the object.

The surrounding air can become glowing plasma.

The object’s surface can heat up, melt, vaporize, and ablate.

Ablation means that material is removed from the surface by extreme heating and high-speed flow.

So yes.

This speed is absolutely enough to create visible atmospheric entry heating.

In everyday language, we often say the object is burning in the atmosphere.

More precisely, it is not ordinary fire.

It is extreme aerodynamic heating, shock-wave heating, plasma formation, and surface ablation.

That is why meteors glow when they enter the atmosphere.
