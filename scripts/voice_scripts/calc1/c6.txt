Now we apply calculus to gravity.

Newton’s law of gravitational interaction describes the force between two masses.

Strictly speaking, both objects attract each other, and both objects can move.

But for this model, we choose the planet as the origin of our coordinate system.

So we describe the asteroid’s motion relative to the planet.

The distance r is the distance from the center of the planet to the center of the asteroid.

Newton’s gravitational formula gives the size of the force.

The force equals G times the planet mass times the asteroid mass, divided by r squared.

Here, G is the gravitational constant.

The planet mass is capital M.

The asteroid mass is small m.

And r is the distance between their centers.

This formula tells us that gravity becomes weaker with distance.

When r becomes larger, r squared grows even faster.

So the force becomes smaller.

But r cannot be zero, because division by zero is undefined.

Physically, this also tells us that we must be careful with the meaning of r.

For a planet and an asteroid, r is not the height above the planet’s surface.

It is the distance from center to center.

If the asteroid is near the surface, then r is approximately the planet radius plus the altitude.

So even at the surface, r is not zero.

It is approximately the radius of the planet.

This also reminds us that Newtonian gravity is a classical model.

It works well for planets, moons, asteroids, satellites, and spacecraft.

But at the subatomic scale, gravity is incredibly weak compared with other interactions.

And at very small distances or extremely high energies, we expect Newton’s formula to be incomplete.

That is the domain of quantum gravity, which is still an open area of physics.

Here we will not consider general relativity, where gravitation is described as the curvature of spacetime rather than as an ordinary force between bodies.
