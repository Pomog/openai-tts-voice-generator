Now we want to find the work done by gravity.

For a constant force, work is simple.

But gravity changes with distance.

So, as before, we use integration.

The general formula for work is a vector formula.

Work equals the integral of force dotted with the tiny displacement vector.

So we write.

W g equals the integral of the gravitational force vector dotted with d r vector.

The dot product is important.

It means that only the part of the force pointing along the motion does work.

Now we define the direction.

The unit vector r-hat points outward from the planet.

It points from the planet’s center toward the asteroid.

But gravity pulls inward.

Gravity pulls the asteroid toward smaller r.

So the gravitational force vector is minus G M m divided by r squared, multiplied by r-hat.

Now consider radial motion.

The asteroid moves directly toward the planet.

So the tiny displacement is also radial.

We write the tiny displacement vector as d r multiplied by r-hat.

Now we substitute this into the work integral.

W g equals the integral from the starting distance r zero to the final distance r f of minus G M m divided by r squared, multiplied by r-hat, dotted with d r multiplied by r-hat.

The dot product of r-hat with r-hat is one.

So the vector expression becomes a scalar integral.

W g equals the integral from r zero to r f of minus G M m divided by r squared, d r.
