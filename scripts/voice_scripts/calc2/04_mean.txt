For a general dataset vector, the squared distance to the constant vector is the sum of the squared differences between each observation and the constant.

This squared distance is now a function of the single unknown value, and we want to find the value that makes it as small as possible.

We differentiate this function and set the derivative equal to zero to find its critical point.

Solving the resulting equation gives the sum of all observations divided by dimensionallity.

This value is exactly the arithmetic mean of the dataset.

The second derivative is positive, so the graph of the squared-distance function is concave up, and the critical point is its unique global minimum.

This result also agrees with the standard vector-projection formula.

The constant subspace is spanned by the all-ones vector.

To project the dataset vector onto this subspace, we take its dot product with the all-ones vector, divide by the squared length of the all-ones vector, and then multiply by the all-ones vector.

The numerator is the sum of all observations, while the denominator is the number of observations, n.

Therefore, the projection is the mean multiplied by the all-ones vector.

The mean of a dataset is the single scalar that, when repeated across all coordinates, forms the projection of the dataset vector onto the constant subspace.
