Opening

We usually think of a dataset as a table, a column, or simply a list of numbers.

But there is another way to see it.

To begin, let us consider one numerical variable: one column containing several observations.

We can treat this entire column as a single vector, with each observation becoming one coordinate.

A dataset with three observations becomes a vector in three-dimensional space.

More generally, a dataset with (n) observations becomes a vector in (n)-dimensional space.

This geometric point of view allows us to reinterpret familiar statistical concepts using vectors.

The mean becomes a projection.

The deviations form a separate vector.

Variance comes from the squared length of that deviation vector.

And correlation becomes the cosine of the angle between two deviation vectors.

We will build these ideas step by step.

Let us begin with the mean.

The Dataset Vector

Here is our example dataset:

[
2,\ 4,\ 6
]

Written as a vector, it becomes:

\begin{bmatrix}
2\
4\
6
\end{bmatrix}
]

Each observation becomes one coordinate of the vector.

Now we want to separate this vector into two parts.

One part will represent the common level of the data.

The other part will represent how each observation differs from that common level.

We will call them the mean component and the deviation component.

So we want to write:

\mathbf{V}{\text{mean}}
+
\mathbf{V}{\text{dev}}
]
