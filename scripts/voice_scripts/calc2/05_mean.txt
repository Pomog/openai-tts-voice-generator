Now let us subtract the projection vector from the original dataset vector.

The resulting remainder is called the deviation vector.

This is the second component of the original dataset vector.

Each of its coordinates tells us how far the corresponding observation lies above or below the mean.

The Euclidean length of the deviation component measures the overall magnitude of variation in the dataset.

If we take the dot product of the deviation vector with itself, we obtain its squared length.

This is the sum of all squared deviations from the mean - total variance.

But variance is not this total.

Variance is the mean of the squared deviations.

To obtain this mean geometrically, we apply the same projection principle once again.

We consider the squared deviations as the coordinates of a new vector and project this vector onto the constant subspace.

Its dot product with the all-ones vector adds all the squared deviations together.

Dividing by the squared length of the all-ones vector converts this total into their mean, because that squared length equals the number of observations.

Finally, multiplying by the all-ones vector produces a constant vector whose common coordinate is the variance.

The square root of that coordinate is the standard deviation.
